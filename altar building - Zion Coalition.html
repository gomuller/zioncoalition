Building a Jesus WORLD: The Practice of Altar Building

A New Kind of Spiritual Gathering

Part 1

Imagine a weekly gathering where people come together not primarily to talk about their struggles, nor simply to discuss ideas, but to build something with Jesus.

This is the idea behind a weekly 40-minute Zoom meeting called “Witting Jesus WORLD Building.”

The meeting is loosely structured like an AA meeting:

  1. 5–10 minute opening reading that sets the frame, expectations, and traditions.
  2. Open share time, where participants speak about what Jesus is building in their lives.

When someone shares, they rhythmically introduce themselves in a way similar to AA:

“Greg, Witting Jesus WORLD Builder.”

The heart of the meeting is not therapy, testimony, or discussion. The heart of the meeting is construction.

Participants gather to share the progress Jesus is making—with their cooperation—in building their personal Jesus WORLD.

The Core Challenge: Avoiding the “Therapy Drift”

When people gather to talk about their relationship with Jesus, the conversation naturally drifts toward:

  • emotional processing
  • life struggles
  • personal stories

These are important, but they easily turn the meeting into a therapy circle.

The intention of the meeting, however, is different.

It is to cultivate a mindset of building.

Instead of sharing only what someone is feeling or experiencing, participants are encouraged to ask:

“What is Jesus building here?”

Even when the insight is personal or abstract, the goal is to move toward something identifiable and constructible.

The Shift: From Experiences to Buildable Things

The key shift in the meeting culture is this:

Instead of sharing experiences, participants share buildable things emerging from those experiences. The meeting explores the idea that spiritual life can generate shareable (replicable) spiritual constructs.

A helpful analogy comes from the technology world.

In tech, people build:

  • apps
  • modules
  • templates
  • systems, protocols
  • reusable components

The goal is that when someone shares, listeners might think:

“I’d like to include that in my Jesus WORLD.”

Just as someone might install an app on their phone.

For example:

Instead of saying:

“Jesus has been teaching me gratitude.”

Someone might say:

“Jesus and I built a Morning Bread practice where I break bread each morning and ask Him one question before eating.”

Now the idea has structure.
It becomes something portable.

A Better Name for the Building Block: The Altar

While technological language like “apps” can be helpful, it also feels somewhat mechanical.

A deeper, more fitting word emerged:

Altar

Throughout scripture, people built altars wherever they encountered God.

  • Abraham built altars.
  • Isaac built altars.
  • Jacob built altars.
  • Moses built altars.

Altars marked places where God acted.

They were physical markers of divine encounter.

In the context of Jesus WORLD building, the idea becomes:

The fundamental building block of a Jesus WORLD is an Altar.

What Is an Altar?

In this framework:

An Altar is a place where a person intentionally meets Jesus and something happens.

An altar may be:

  • physical
  • symbolic
  • ritual
  • conversational
  • situational

But it always has structure.

A Jesus WORLD can therefore be imagined as:

A landscape filled with altars.

Each altar becomes a node of relationship with Jesus.

The Basic Structure of an Altar

Altars can be described using a few simple components.

1. The Place

Where the altar exists.

Examples:

  • kitchen table
  • walking trail
  • car commute
  • journal
  • bedside chair
  • work desk

2. The Action

What happens there.

Examples:

  • breaking bread
  • asking Jesus a question
  • silent listening
  • confession
  • gratitude
  • prayer for others

3. The Object (optional)

Physical or symbolic elements.

Examples:

  • bread
  • candle
  • stone
  • cup
  • notebook

4. The Pattern

The repeatable rule of the altar.

Example:

“Before eating breakfast, I ask Jesus what He wants to build today.”

Examples of Altars

The Bread Altar

Place: Kitchen table
Object: Bread
Pattern: Break bread and ask Jesus a question before eating.

Share example:

“Greg, Witting Jesus WORLD Builder.
Jesus and I started building a Bread Altar in my kitchen this week.”

The Commute Altar

Place: Car
Pattern: First five minutes of the drive belong to Jesus.

Share example:

“Jesus and I built a Commute Altar where the first five minutes of driving are silent listening.”

The Anger Altar

Place: Moments of conflict
Pattern: Pause and ask Jesus before responding.

Share example:

“Jesus and I are building an Anger Altar where I stop and ask Him how to respond.”

The Morning Silence Altar

Place: Bedside or kitchen chair
Pattern: Three minutes of silence before checking the phone.

Why “Altar” Works So Well

Using the word altar solves several important challenges.

It gives the practice:

Spiritual depth

Altars are ancient, biblical, and sacred.

Embodiment

Altars are places and practices, not just ideas.

Structure

They have boundaries and patterns.

Shareability

Someone can hear about an altar and decide:

“I want that altar in my Jesus WORLD.”

The Culture of the Meeting

The meeting gradually develops a culture centered around a simple question:

“What altar is Jesus building in your world?”

Even when someone shares a personal struggle, the conversation can gently move toward construction:

“What altar might Jesus be building there?”

In this way, personal experiences become raw material for spiritual architecture.

Layers of Construction

Over time, the structure becomes clearer.

Level 1 — Altars

The fundamental unit.

Level 2 — Altar Components

Objects, actions, and patterns.

Level 3 — Altar Networks

Groups of altars forming rhythms.

Example morning network:

  • Silence Altar
  • Bread Altar
  • Listening Altar

Level 4 — Jesus WORLD

The full landscape of altars forming a life with Jesus.

What the (meeting) Shares Sound Like

A typical share might sound like this:

“Greg, Witting Jesus WORLD Builder.

This week Jesus and I started building a Small Silence Altar in my mornings.
It’s just three minutes of silence before I check my phone.”

Or:

“Jesus helped me build a Forgiveness Altar in my marriage this week.”

Or:

“I’m experimenting with a Pause Before Email Altar where I ask Jesus one question before replying.”

These shares are simple, concrete, and buildable.

The Long-Term Vision

Over time, the community may create an expanding library of altars.

Examples might include:

  • Morning Silence Altar
  • Bread Altar
  • Commute Altar
  • Gratitude Stone Altar
  • Listening Walk Altar
  • Email Pause Altar
  • Forgiveness Altar
  • Work Desk Altar

Each one represents a small technology of relationship with Jesus.

Participants can adopt, adapt, and expand them within their own Jesus WORLD.

A Gathering of Altar Builders

The meeting ultimately becomes something quite unique:

A group of people gathering weekly to witness the altars Jesus is building in their worlds.

Instead of simply discussing faith, they are architects of lived relationship.

They are builders of landscapes of encounter.

And each week they come together and say, in rhythm:

“Greg, Witting Jesus WORLD Builder.”

Then they share what Jesus is building next.

Part 2

Jesus Intends To Have Your Whole WORLD Be a “Jesus Altar”

(One big singular altar with a series of mini-altars that run throughout the day)

Introduction

Over the last decade, Jesus has made clear His intention for every person on Earth as He builds their respective world. This episode describes that divine aim.

“Jesus Intends To Have My Whole WORLD Be a Jesus Altar”

Your entire world—your thoughts, actions, spaces, and creations—becomes a consecrated zone of communion. Nothing is “ordinary” anymore; every moment turns into an offering and an exchange between you and Jesus.

It’s not just about bringing something to the altar; it’s about being the altar—living as the meeting point where Jesus and creation touch.

Macro–Altar: The One Big Singular Altar

Think of this as your Jesus–Greg WORLD—the grand design Jesus and you are co-building.

  • It is the cathedral of your days, the ecosystem of devotion.
  • Every place you walk, every project you build, every word you speak becomes part of that structure.
  • Jesus’ presence is the fire on that altar—the living flame that never goes out.

This mirrors the Old Testament image of the altar fire that must never be extinguished (Leviticus 6:13) and Paul’s invitation: “Present your bodies as a living sacrifice… which is your spiritual worship.” (Romans 12:1)

Micro–Altars: The Mini-Altars Throughout Your Day

These are the touchpoints of practice—the moments when you pause, lift, notice, or surrender.

  • Morning altar: first thoughts, a breath offered to Jesus before speaking.
  • Work altar: dedicating each project as worship—“Jesus, be in this.”
  • Meal altar: gratitude as communion.
  • Conversation altar: listening as an act of love.
  • Evening altar: reviewing the day, releasing all outcomes to Him.

Each one is a spark, and together they keep your world glowing with His presence—like a constellation of devotion.

Purpose

The purpose of this world-altar pattern is:

  • Integration – removing the divide between sacred and secular.
  • Constant awareness – living in continual prayer.
  • Transformation – everything placed on the altar is changed by love.

As Jesus co-authors my Jesus–Greg WORLD—this Big Jesus Altar—His guiding question for every mini-altar is:

“How can we fill this scene with “MORE Jesus”—more Jesus themes, more Jesus memories, more Jesus reflections?”

Each altar becomes a doorway for more Jesus to enter and dwell within my world.

If money were the center, the guiding question would be:

“How can I get more money—more money themes, more money experiences—into my altar?”

But because Jesus is the center, the question shifts to:

“How can I get more Jesus—more Jesus themes, more Jesus memories, more Jesus reflections—into this altar?”

That single change of center transforms the entire world-altar—
from accumulation to communion,
from possession to presence,
from earning to adoring.

Language You Could Use

If you want to name this practice poetically, a few phrases that capture it might be:

  • Living-Altar Mode
  • 24-Hour Worship Cycle
  • Jesus-World Continuum
  • Everywhere-is-Altar
  • My Whole-World Offering

ADDENDUM

An altar is a sacred space or structure—often a table, platform, or designated area—used for worship, offering, prayer, and connection with the divine. It’s one of the oldest religious symbols across cultures and faiths, representing a meeting point between heaven and earth, human and divine, or seen and unseen realms.

1. Definition

An altar is a consecrated place set apart for sacred acts. It can be:

  • A physical structure (stone, wood, metal, etc.)
  • A symbolic or personal space (e.g., a prayer corner, a virtual altar)
  • A spiritual posture—a state of offering oneself to God

2. Core Purposes

a. Offering and Sacrifice

  • Historically, altars were where people offered gifts or sacrifices to God (e.g., Noah, Abraham, and Moses).
  • In Christianity, Jesus’ sacrifice on the cross is the ultimate altar moment, fulfilling all others.

b. Communion with the Divine

  • The altar serves as a meeting place—a bridge between human and divine.
  • In Christian worship, the Eucharist (Communion) is consecrated at the altar, symbolizing direct fellowship with Christ.

c. Remembrance and Covenant

  • Altars often mark divine encounters or covenants (e.g., Jacob at Bethel).
  • They stand as memorials of when heaven touched earth.

d. Prayer and Intercession

  • Many traditions use altars as focal points for prayer and thanksgiving.
  • Lighting candles, burning incense, or laying offerings symbolize ongoing connection with God or ancestors.

3. Symbolic Meaning

An altar often symbolizes:

  • Surrender – giving something fully to God.
  • Transformation – what is placed there is changed or redeemed.
  • Presence – God’s nearness.
  • Devotion – love and loyalty.

4. Modern Expressions

Today, altars appear in many forms:

  • In churches → for Communion or worship.
  • In homes → as prayer tables or meditation spaces.
  • In personal spirituality → journals, objects, or even digital altars within virtual or world-building contexts.

In short:
 An altar is where you bring what matters most to you and meet God in that offering.
It’s where your world touches His—a place of exchange, remembrance, and transformation.

Part 3 – Why This?

SWEET FAILURE.

Most people do not change simply because a better idea exists. We change when the way we are living stops working for us in a meaningful and sustained way. When our old patterns fail long enough or deeply enough, they push us to experiment with new paths, new practices, and new ways of seeing the world…. Around 2015, something deep stopped working for me…and  I had what some people call a midlife crisis.  (What a still small voice in me calls a “born again” experience.) Regardless, for the past decade plus I have wondered at the mystery that is the new  me, the changed me. 

Why have I so dramatically changed the way I approach Jesus? 

Why do I have all this energy for building what I call a “Jesus WORLD” and creating these new “Jesus Altars.

There’s actually many ways I could answer that. but the most simple, and satisfying answer is: what I was doing before wasn’t working for me.  It stopped working for me.

(And I realized that what I was doing before still works for other people.  It hasn’t failed them.  Maybe it won’t. And certainly  they won’t change until what works for them stops working.)

BOTTOM LINE: People usually keep doing whatever they believe works. If something gives us the results we want in life or faith, we stick with it. But when it stops working, we start looking for another path.

That’s what happened for me. The way I had been practicing my faith wasn’t producing the transformation or connection I was looking for. So I began experimenting with a different relationship with Jesus—one expressed through altars and forms of worship that are unconventional, even sacred-clown in nature.

And to my surprise, that approach started working better for me. It created a deeper sense of connection and life in my faith. So I kept following that path.

(I like to call it “The Jesus-Greg Covenant Path.”  I like believing that each person has their own personal Jesus-Covenant Path to walk.  One size fits one….. I may be wrong. That’s just a belief that I’m willing to die for.)

Here is a companion piece that keeps the tone and philosophy of your first section while flowing naturally beneath it.

MORE JESUS

As I continued walking what I call the Jesus–Greg Covenant Path, another idea slowly began to take shape in my mind.

I began to suspect that Jesus might function like a kind of spiritual singularity—a center point where everything bends toward life, meaning, and healing. In my experience, the closer something moves toward that center, the better it tends to become.

In simple terms, I started forming a working hypothesis: things get better with more Jesus.

So I turned that idea into an experiment.

If more Jesus really does make things better, then perhaps the practical question becomes: Where in my life could more Jesus be intentionally added? Where could His presence, attention, gratitude, humor, reverence, or remembrance be woven more deeply into the ordinary structures of my days?

That question is what motivated me to start building what I call Jesus Altars and shaping what I think of as a Jesus World. These structures are simply ways of noticing important parts of my life—relationships, work, creativity, struggles, hopes—and intentionally blending more Jesus into them.

Each altar becomes a small focusing point, a reminder, a way of directing attention and devotion toward something that matters.

In a sense, I’m running a long-term experiment: What happens when you deliberately add more Jesus to everything?

So far, the results have been encouraging. The experiment seems to be working.

Life feels more alive, more connected, and more meaningful when I consciously bring Jesus into more of it.

So for now, I’m continuing the experiment.

Adding more Jesus.
Building more altars.
Expanding the Jesus World.

“…and the labor which they had to perform was to look” (1 Nephi 17:41)

We look to Christ, which leads us to think of Christ, “And we talk of Christ, we rejoice in Christ, we preach of Christ, we prophesy of Christ, and we write according to our prophecies, that our children may know to what source they may look…” (2 Nephi 25:26)

We lookers. Human beings instinctually look at/ pay attention to (=worship). That which we consistently look at will eventually have power to POSSESS us (get inside us, Alma 34:34). For good, or for bad.

We build to “feed our heads”. Jesus leads us to build altars as places to look toward Him; what we behold shapes us (possesses us). Unlike Babylon’s altars, these “Jesus-themed altars” form us in His image—-possession by Him, not possession by the world. Possession by Jesus’ Spirit feels good (at very least it feels better than Babylon Spirit). That’s ultimately why we do it.

Essay

We Are Lookers

What we look at will shape what we expect. What we expect will shape how we live.

Human beings are, at their core, lookers. We are creatures of attention. We are always looking at something—externally with our eyes, internally with our thoughts. And what we consistently look at, we do not merely observe; we begin to absorb. Attention is not passive. It is formative. In a real sense, what we look at becomes what we love, what we love shapes what we believe, and what we believe shapes who we become.

This is why it is not unreasonable to describe attention as a kind of worship. To “worship” something, in its most basic sense, is to give it sustained attention and value. Whether we intend it or not, whatever occupies our gaze—our mental and emotional focus—begins to enter us. It forms our instincts, our reactions, and eventually our character. This aligns with the idea in Alma 34:34: what we allow into ourselves does not remain external. It becomes part of us. Over time, it can even “possess” us—not necessarily in some dramatic sense, but in the quiet, incremental way that habits of thought become the lens through which we see everything.

But humans are not only lookers. We are also anticipators. We are constantly projecting forward, asking—consciously or unconsciously—“What comes next?” In this sense, every human being engages in a form of prophecy. Not necessarily in the sacred or revelatory sense, but in the everyday cognitive sense: we are always predicting outcomes, imagining consequences, and constructing possible futures.

This is one reason stories, especially good ones, are so compelling. A well-told story captures our attention and activates our anticipatory nature. As we watch or read, we begin to form hypotheses: If this happens, then that will follow. “Oh, I think I see where this is going.” A good story invites prediction, then reshapes or fulfills it in a way that is both surprising and satisfying. When the ending feels right—when good overcomes evil, when justice is restored, when meaning emerges—we experience a deep sense of satisfaction. Not merely because the plot resolved, but because it aligned with an internal expectation we carry: that reality, at some level, is meaningful and ordered.

This reveals something profound. Humans do not just predict events; we predict meaning. We expect, or at least hope, that things will ultimately make sense—that chaos can be resolved, that suffering is not the final word, that good can triumph over evil. This expectation operates like an internal narrative template: struggle, conflict, and uncertainty are followed, ideally, by resolution, restoration, and meaning.

Attention and Anticipation

Here is where the two ideas—attention and anticipation—intersect. What we consistently look at shapes what we come to expect. And what we expect shapes how we interpret and experience reality. In other words, our attention trains our inner “prophecy engine.”

If we consistently focus on threat, failure, betrayal, and disorder, we begin to anticipate those things everywhere. We start to “see” a future filled with breakdown and disappointment. On the other hand, if we attend to goodness, truth, resilience, and redemption—even in small forms—we begin to expect that meaning can emerge, that things can be worked out, that good is not powerless.

Faith and Anxiety

This is where the contrast between anxiety and faith becomes clear.

Anxiety is, at its core, a form of negative prophecy. It is the habit of anticipating undesirable outcomes and treating them as likely or inevitable. It says, “This will go badly,” “I will fail,” “Something will fall apart.” Importantly, anxiety often disguises these predictions as facts. But if we look closely, we can see that anxiety is not simply a reaction to reality—it is a projection about the future. It is, in a sense, misplaced faith: faith that things will go wrong.

Faith, by contrast, is not the denial of difficulty or the insistence that everything will be easy. Rather, it is a different kind of anticipation. It is the willingness to believe that even if something is hard, it is not meaningless; even if things go wrong, that is not the final outcome; even if the path is unclear, there is still the possibility of resolution, growth, or redemption. Faith is trust-oriented prophecy. It does not eliminate uncertainty, but it refuses to collapse into despair.

Both anxiety and faith rely on the same human capacities: attention, pattern recognition, and prediction. The difference lies in what those capacities have been trained on. If our attention has been dominated by negative patterns, our anticipations will reflect that. If our attention has been shaped by examples of goodness and meaning, our expectations will be different.

Anxiety says the future is doomed. Faith says the future is not yet finished.

This leads to a crucial practical insight: we are not passive observers of our mental life. We are participants in its formation. The loop is constant—attention shapes anticipation, anticipation shapes experience, and experience reinforces attention—but it is not fixed. It can be influenced.

The first step is awareness. When we feel anxiety, we can ask: “What am I expecting to happen?” This exposes the hidden prophecy. Next, we can distinguish between what is actually happening and what we are predicting. Often, much of our distress comes not from present reality but from imagined outcomes.

From there, we can challenge the assumption that there is only one possible future. There are always multiple plausible outcomes, and recognizing this loosens the grip of anxiety. Then, instead of forcing artificial optimism, we can adopt a stance of grounded faith: “I do not know exactly how this will turn out, but I do not have to assume the worst. Even if it is difficult, it may still lead somewhere meaningful.”

At the same time, we can intentionally direct our attention. What we repeatedly focus on matters. Attending to examples of goodness, competence, truth, and redemption is not naïve—it is formative. It trains our perception and reshapes our expectations. Finally, action plays a decisive role. When we act despite anxious predictions, we generate new experiences that can update our internal models. Over time, this creates a feedback loop in which healthier attention leads to more constructive anticipation, which leads to more courageous action, which in turn reinforces a more hopeful and grounded view of reality.

The Christian Fulfillment

In the end, we cannot escape being lookers and we cannot escape being prophets. We are always attending, and we are always anticipating. The question is not whether these processes are happening, but what is shaping them.

What we look at will shape what we expect.
What we expect will shape how we live.

So the enduring question is: What are we allowing to train our vision of the future—and who, as a result, are we becoming?

This is one reason the invitations of Jesus matter so much. He does not merely tell us what to do; He tells us where to look and what to anticipate. He repeatedly commands His disciples to watch, to look, to seek, to abide, and to lift up their eyes and hearts. He teaches us to watch for His coming, to be ready, to stay awake, to live as people who expect the Bridegroom, the Master, the King to return. In other words, Christ commands a redeemed kind of attention. He asks us to become holy lookers.

And He also commands a redeemed kind of anticipation. He does not want His people dragged helplessly along by anxious forecasts, by the world’s prophecies of doom, or by their own fears. He wants us anticipating Him. The Second Coming is therefore not just a doctrine about the future; it is a discipline for the present. To watch for Christ is to let the promised ending shape the present moment. It is to live now in the light of what is surely coming. As we look to Him, we learn what to love. As we anticipate Him, we learn what to expect. And slowly, by grace, our attention and our prophecy are both converted.

As we look to Him, we learn what to love. As we anticipate Him, we learn what to expect.
