Interestingly, history provides a useful approximation.

The gathering that ultimately launched the King James Bible was not itself a translation committee.

It was a meeting.

A conversation.

An idea.

A proposal.

In January 1604, King James convened what became known as the Hampton Court Conference.

During that gathering, a Puritan scholar proposed a new English translation of the Bible.

King James approved the idea.

From that seed emerged a project involving dozens of scholars working for years.

Eventually, the King James Version was published in 1611.

The conference itself did not produce the Bible.

The conference launched the effort.

The Bible emerged later.

Line upon line.

Scholar upon scholar.

Page upon page.

Year upon year.
